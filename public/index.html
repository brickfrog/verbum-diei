Margin

Marginalia

  1. R 1,2Elijah rendered as combustion: prophet = ignition; speech = heat.
  2. R 3,4,5,6Power over scarcity and sky; judgment enacted by withdrawal and fire.
  3. R 9,10Exit is not death but transport; fire frames both ministry and departure.
  4. R 11,12Stored as a future instrument: reproof timed to avert wrath.
  5. R 13,14Restoration defined as relational reversal (fathers/sons) and political repair (tribes).
  6. R 15,16,17Beatitude shifts from Elijah’s glory to witnesses’ beautification; life promised by proximity.
  7. G 1,2Theophany sealed under a resurrection embargo; disclosure is scheduled, not denied.
  8. G 3Scribes’ sequence problem: Elijah first, then kingdom.
  9. G 5,6Two claims held together: Elijah “will restore” and “has come” unrecognized—restoration arrives in misrecognition and violence.
  10. G 6Typology tightened: what is done to the forerunner prefigures what will be done to the Son of Man.
  11. G 7Identification collapses the expectation: Elijah = John, not a returned fire-chariot.

A reading from the Book of Sirach

Sirach 48:1-4,9-11
World English Bible — Public Domain

1Also there arose Elijah the prophet as fire,

2And his word burned like a torch:

3Who brought a famine upon them,

4And by his zeal made them few in number.

5By the word of the Lord he shut up the heaven:

6Thrice did he thus bring down fire.

7How was you glorified, O Elijah, in your wondrous deeds!

8And who shall glory like to you?

9Who was taken up in a tempest of fire,

10In a chariot of fiery horses:

11Who was recorded for reproofs in their seasons,

12To pacify anger, before it brake forth into wrath;

13To turn the heart of the father to the son,

14And to restore the tribes of Jacob.

15Blessed are those who saw you,

16And those who have been beautified with love;

17For we also shall surely live.

Gloss

Commentary

On the Reading
  1. R 1,2Elijah is described as fire; the text treats prophetic speech as an element that consumes.
  2. R 3,4,5,6The prophet’s authority is cosmic (heaven shut) and punitive (famine, descending fire) but explicitly derivative: “by the word of the Lord.”
  3. R 9,10Elijah’s translation in fire becomes part of his profile, not merely an ending.
  4. R 11,12,13,14Elijah is positioned as an eschatological lever: reproof, anger-pacification, household reconciliation, tribal restoration.
On the Gospel
  1. G 1,2Jesus controls testimony: the vision’s meaning is unintelligible apart from resurrection.
  2. G 3,5,6The Elijah expectation is affirmed and reinterpreted: the restoring figure came as one who could be ignored and harmed; restoration is not triumphal.
  3. G 6,7John’s fate becomes the interpretive key and the template for the Son of Man’s suffering.

Sirach casts Elijah as fiery judgment and future restoration; Matthew relocates that role into John the Baptizer, showing restoration arriving under concealment, misrecognition, and suffering, with revelation deferred until resurrection.
