Margin

Marginalia

  1. R 1,2Fire as idiom: presence that consumes; speech as ignition.
  2. R 3,5,6Nature weaponized by obedience: famine, sealed heaven, answered fire—judgment by decree, not force.
  3. R 7,8Rhetorical exaltation; the text manufactures singularity to fix memory.
  4. R 9,10Ascent without decay: translation marks a life unresolved by death; claims of access to the court of God.
  5. R 11,12“Recorded” shifts Elijah into an eschatological instrument: rebuke timed to history’s crises.
  6. R 13,14Restoration defined as kinship repaired and polity reconstituted; covenant politics, not sentiment.
  7. R 15,17Beatitude of witnesses; life promised by association with the prophetic economy.
  8. G 1,2Commanded secrecy: vision is unintelligible until resurrection rewires categories.
  9. G 3,5Scribal expectation acknowledged, then redirected: “restore” becomes contested fulfillment.
  10. G 6Misrecognition is permitted and culpable; prophetic figure can be treated as disposable—pattern for the Son of Man.
  11. G 7Identification collapses typology: Elijah-function executed by John; fulfillment arrives as repetition with violence.
Reading

A reading from the Book of Sirach

Sirach 48:1-4,9-11
World English Bible — Public Domain

1Also there arose Elijah the prophet as fire,

2And his word burned like a torch:

3Who brought a famine upon them,

4And by his zeal made them few in number.

5By the word of the Lord he shut up the heaven:

6Thrice did he thus bring down fire.

7How was you glorified, O Elijah, in your wondrous deeds!

8And who shall glory like to you?

9Who was taken up in a tempest of fire,

10In a chariot of fiery horses:

11Who was recorded for reproofs in their seasons,

12To pacify anger, before it brake forth into wrath;

13To turn the heart of the father to the son,

14And to restore the tribes of Jacob.

15Blessed are those who saw you,

16And those who have been beautified with love;

17For we also shall surely live.

Gloss

Commentary

On the Reading
  1. R 1,2Elijah is rendered as elemental speech: the prophet is not merely messenger but event.
  2. R 3,6Authority appears as command over scarcity and sky; the prophet mediates covenant sanctions.
  3. R 9,14His “departure” secures future return in imagination; the text assigns him a role in turning wrath and repairing lineage and tribes.
  4. R 15,17The closing beatitude ties survival to having seen—vision as credential within salvation history.
On the Gospel
  1. G 1,2The transfiguration is sealed until the resurrection makes disclosure non-misleading.
  2. G 3,6Jesus accepts the Elijah schema yet says it has already occurred in a form the public could brutalize; restoration and suffering coexist.
  3. G 7John is named as Elijah by function, not identity; the pattern forecasts Jesus’ reception.

Sirach canonizes Elijah as fire and future corrective; Matthew claims the corrective arrived as John and was rejected, making “restoration” inseparable from misrecognition and violence, and tying Elijah’s return to the passion logic rather than triumphal repair.
